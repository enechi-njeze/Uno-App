# 1.  The problem

## The fraud number

Nigerian property fraud is estimated at roughly $4bn annually, with the majority tied to defective, forged, or duplicated title. Roughly 3% of Nigerians hold a valid registered land title, and formal registration is slow, expensive, and concentrated in urban centres. The World Bank has consistently ranked Nigeria near the bottom globally on registering property.

[ESTIMATE — verify before external use] These figures circulate widely in Nigerian real estate press and industry commentary. Before this doc goes to a partner firm or an investor, each should be traced to a primary source (World Bank Doing Business archives, NBS, Lagos State Lands Bureau, or a named industry report) and footnoted. An unsourced $4bn in a pitch deck is a liability; a sourced $4bn is a weapon.

## What actually goes wrong

The fraud is not exotic. It is a small set of repeated patterns:

| Pattern | What happens |
| --- | --- |
| Multiple sale | One parcel sold to several buyers, each with plausible-looking documents |
| Forged or upgraded title | A Letter of Allocation presented as if it carried the weight of a C of O |
| Government acquisition land | Land sold inside a committed acquisition zone; buyer discovers only at development |
| Omo-onile extortion | Family and community claimants demand repeat payments after purchase, particularly in Lagos |
| Ghost listing | Property does not exist, is not available, or is not the seller's — photos lifted from other listings |
| Agent chain inflation | Three or four intermediaries each adding a fee, none disclosed |
| Rent fraud | Advance paid for a property the "agent" does not control |

Every one of these is a verification failure at the point of transaction, not a discovery failure. That distinction is the whole strategy.

## Why the existing platforms do not solve it

Nigeria's incumbent portals — PropertyPro, Nigeria Property Centre, PrivateProperty, Jiji's property vertical, plus the informal but dominant layer of Facebook groups and WhatsApp broadcast lists — are classified-ad businesses. They monetise agent subscriptions and listing prominence. Their revenue improves as listing volume rises, which means the business model is structurally hostile to verification: every listing you reject is revenue you declined.

Nobody has built a Nigerian portal where verification is the product rather than an overhead.

# 2.  The wedge

Do not police the conversation. Own the settlement.

Unö does not try to prevent agents and buyers from talking outside the platform — that fight is unwinnable in a WhatsApp-first market. Instead, Unö makes leaving the platform mean losing three things simultaneously: verified title status, escrowed money, and contract protection.

Phase 1 delivers only the first of those. That is sufficient to prove the model and win partners, and it is deliberately below the security line.

Positioning in one line, for a partner meeting:

Every other Nigerian property platform sells attention. Unö sells certainty.

# 3.  Target users

### Primary — Diaspora buyer

Nigerians in the UK, US, and Canada buying land or property at home. Highest fraud exposure (cannot inspect, rely on relatives and agents), highest willingness to pay for verification, and highest transaction value. They already pay for informal versions of this — flying home, sending cousins to look, hiring lawyers ad hoc.

What they need: proof the property exists, proof the title is real, proof the agent is real, and a way to watch the deal progress from 5,000 miles away.

[ASSUMPTION] Diaspora is the most valuable early segment. Worth validating with 15–20 conversations before Phase 3 spend.

### Primary — Domestic upper-middle buyer (Abuja / Lagos)

Professionals buying their first serious asset, typically land or an off-plan unit, often on an installment plan. They know someone who has been defrauded. They are price-sensitive on the platform but not on protection.

### Primary — Renter (Abuja / Lagos)

Highest volume, lowest value, but the fee-transparency problem hits them hardest: annual rent quoted, one to two years demanded upfront, plus agency fee, caution fee, legal/agreement fee, and service charge — frequently disclosed only after they have committed emotionally. The Total Cost of Acquisition ledger is a renter product as much as a buyer one, and renters are the acquisition funnel for future buyers.

### Supply side — Verified agents and firms

The honest agent is currently indistinguishable from the fraudulent one. That is the pitch: Unö is the only place where being legitimate is a competitive advantage rather than a cost disadvantage. Target the firm before the individual — Nigerians trust J.U. Uzor & Associates before they trust "Emeka, estate agent."

### Supply side — Developers

Off-plan and installment sales are dominant. Developers want qualified, verified buyers and a credible sales channel. They are also the most likely early paying customer for premium placement and verified inspection reports.

# 4.  Competitive landscape

| Player | Model | Where they are strong | Where Unö wins |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| PropertyPro / NPC / PrivateProperty | Agent-subscription classifieds | Inventory volume, SEO, brand recognition | No verification; revenue model punishes rejecting listings |
| Jiji (property) | General marketplace | Enormous traffic, free listings | Zero trust infrastructure; buyer beware is explicit |
| Facebook groups / WhatsApp lists | Informal, free | Where deals actually happen; zero friction | No record, no recourse, primary fraud vector |
| Individual firms (incl. J.U. Uzor) | Offline, relationship-based | Real trust, real diligence | No reach, no digital surface, no scale |
| Unö | Free listings, paid verification & settlement | Verification as product; firm-centric; diaspora-native | Must build inventory from zero |

The honest read on competition: Unö's disadvantage is inventory and traffic. Incumbents have years of both. Unö's advantage is that their business model cannot follow it — a classifieds business that starts rejecting listings destroys its own revenue. This is a structural moat, not a feature moat, which is the only kind worth having.

Second structural moat: every completed verification adds to a proprietary verified-title database. No competitor can replicate that without doing the same work, and the work is legal and human, not technical. Over time this becomes an asset independent of the app.

# 5.  Business model

## Core principle

Listings are free, forever. This is a strategic commitment, not a launch promo. Charging for listings recreates the incumbent's incentive problem — it makes volume the metric. Free listings let Unö reject aggressively without revenue pain, which is the only way verification stays credible.

Revenue comes from services that are only possible because verification exists.

## Revenue lines

| # | Line | Who pays | Phase | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Title verification | Buyer or owner | 1 | Solicitor-backed registry check + risk report. The flagship paid service. |
| 2 | Verified inspection report | Buyer (esp. diaspora) | 1 | Vetted surveyor visits, photographs, certifies listing accuracy. Converts the professional network into product. |
| 3 | Agent / firm verification badge | Agent or firm | 1 | Annual KYC + credential check. Modest fee; the badge is the value. |
| 4 | "Verify your property" | Owner | 1–2 | Owner verifies title pre-listing; builds the proprietary title database. |
| 5 | Escrow settlement fee | Split buyer / seller | 2 | % of transaction, via licensed partner. Becomes the dominant line at scale. |
| 6 | Contract generation + e-sign | Transacting parties | 2 | Bundled with escrow in practice. |
| 7 | Premium listing (3D + verified + escrow-ready) | Agent / developer | 2 | Zillow's premium is prettier photos; Unö's premium is provable. |
| 8 | Landlord / property-management subscription | Landlord | 3 | Annual rent cycle, service charge, renewals, maintenance. Strong retention. |
| 9 | Diaspora concierge / remote purchase | Diaspora buyer | 3 | Highest-margin, highest-trust bundle. |
| 10 | Data & valuation products | Institutions, developers, lenders | 4 | Only viable once real settled-price data accumulates. |

Explicitly refused revenue:

- Paid search ranking. Ranking is by verification tier and completed-transaction quality only. Selling placement quietly would poison the one asset the company has. If ever monetised, it must be labelled unmistakably.

- Becoming a lender or fund custodian. Escrow is a licensed partner integration. Holding funds makes Unö a regulated financial institution — a different company entirely.

## Unit economics sketch

The shape of the model matters more than the numbers right now:

- Title verification has a real marginal cost (solicitor time, registry search fees, sometimes physical registry attendance). Price must cover that cost plus margin, and the price ceiling is set by what a buyer would otherwise pay a lawyer directly — Unö must be cheaper and faster than the status quo, or it is just a middleman.

- Inspection reports are similar: surveyor time, travel, report production.

- Agent badges are near-zero marginal cost after the first check — this is the line that scales.

- Escrow (Phase 2) is percentage-based on high-value transactions and is where the business actually becomes large.

The one number that needs to be true: verification must be deliverable at a price a normal Abuja buyer will pay, at a turnaround measured in days, not weeks. If the realistic answer is "three weeks and several hundred thousand naira," the productisation problem is harder than the software problem, and that changes the roadmap. This is the single most important question to resolve in the Nigeria meeting.

# 6.  Phased plan

## Phase 1 — Trust MVP (current build)

Goal: prove the verified-listing model works, and produce a demo compelling enough to close Julie and recruit a technical co-founder.

Scope: listings + media, landmark search, Trust Panel, transparent fee ledger, in-app chat + WhatsApp, inspection booking, agent/listing verification, admin review panel.

Not in scope: escrow, contracts/e-sign, 3D scanning, multi-currency, valuation, property management.

No customer money moves through Phase 1. This is deliberate — it keeps the build below the security line and allows AI-assisted development without exposing anyone’s funds.

Success criteria:

- A working end-to-end demo on a real phone

- A defined, repeatable verification workflow agreed with the anchor firm

- [ASSUMPTION] A pilot cohort of roughly 50–100 genuinely verified listings across Abuja and Lagos

- [ASSUMPTION] 5–10 verified agents, or one anchor firm's full inventory

- A signed or agreed partnership with J.U. Uzor & Associates

Volume targets are deliberately small. Fifty listings that are genuinely verified beat five thousand that are not — the latter makes Unö indistinguishable from PropertyPro.

## Phase 2 — Transaction rail

Gate: an experienced engineer on the team and a completed security review. Non-negotiable.

Scope: licensed escrow partner integration, contract generation + e-sign with tamper-evident audit log, the nine-stage milestone engine, 3D capture, dispute tooling.

This is where the wedge closes and where revenue becomes significant. It is also where regulatory exposure begins, which is why the gate exists.

## Phase 3 — Scale & diaspora

Multi-currency wallet and FX-financed purchase, remote inspection, comparable-price model evolving toward a true Unö Estimate, financing hub (installment, cooperative, NHF, rent-to-own), landlord and property-management portal. City expansion beyond Abuja and Lagos — most plausibly Port Harcourt and Ibadan.

## Phase 4 — Infrastructure

Unö stops being an app and becomes a rail: the verified-title database as a standalone product, valuation data sold to developers and lenders, verification-as-a-service for other platforms. This is the endgame that justifies the patience of phases 1–3, but it only exists if the transaction history from Phase 2 accumulates.

# 7.  The partnership play

## Why J.U. Uzor & Associates is the anchor

Unö's product is legal certainty. Legal certainty is produced by lawyers, not by software. A trust platform without a credible legal backbone is a claim; with one it is a service. Julie's firm — with AEGIS and her legal partners providing the verification backbone — supplies exactly the thing Unö cannot build in code.

Secondarily, this is a legacy project: reviving a late father’s estate-management firm digitally. That is not a business argument, but it is the reason this partnership is the right one rather than merely a convenient one, and it should be said plainly in the room.

## What each side brings

| Unö brings | J.U. Uzor / AEGIS brings |
| --- | --- |
| Digital surface, reach, diaspora channel | Verification capability (registry search, title opinion) |
| Product, engineering, design | Professional credibility and regulatory standing |
| Distribution and demand generation | Existing inventory and client relationships |
| Data infrastructure and the title database | Solicitor and surveyor network |
| Firm-branded profile pages with full inventory | Domain judgment on what a real title check requires |

## The offer

[ASSUMPTION — commercial terms are yours to set; this frames the shape, not the numbers]

Anchor partner and equity co-founder entity, meaning:

- Equity in Unö at a level reflecting co-founder status, with vesting tied to delivery of verification capacity.

- Exclusive anchor firm status in the launch cities for a defined term — the first branded firm profile, prominent placement, and reference-partner positioning.

- Verification revenue share on every paid verification the firm executes. This makes the partnership self-funding rather than a favour.

- Named legal authority on the Trust Panel: "Verified by J.U. Uzor & Associates" appearing on listings the firm has cleared. Reputational asset for both sides.

- Input rights on verification standards — the firm defines what a verification actually means, because they carry the professional risk.

## What to ask for in the Nigeria meeting

Ordered by importance, not by ease:

- What does a real title verification cost and take, in days and naira? Everything downstream depends on this answer.

- What is the minimum documentary standard the firm would stake its name on? This becomes the verification tier definition in the product.

- Would the firm let its inventory be the seed catalogue for launch — the first 50 listings?

- Which partners can cover Lagos, given the firm's centre of gravity? Lagos verification is a different registry and a different network.

- Is she in, and on what terms?

## Risk in the partnership itself

Concentration. If Unö's verification depends entirely on one firm, the firm's capacity is Unö's ceiling and the firm's departure is an existential event. The mitigation is not to hedge against Julie — it is to design the verification workflow as a standard other firms can also meet, so that firm two and firm three are additive rather than threatening. Say this openly in the meeting; a partner who understands they are the first of several, with anchor privileges, is more comfortable than one who suspects they are being locked in.

| # | Risk | Severity | Mitigation |

# 9.  What has to be true

The roadmap rests on four load-bearing assumptions. If any is false, the plan changes materially — worth stating them plainly so they can be tested rather than assumed.

- Verification is productisable. A title check can be delivered in days, at a price a normal buyer will pay. Test: the Nigeria meeting.

- Buyers will pay for certainty. Enough of the market values verified over cheap. Test: first 20 paid verifications.

- Good agents want to be distinguishable. Legitimate agents will accept scrutiny for the advantage of a badge. Test: agent recruitment in Phase 1.

- The anchor partnership closes. Test: two weeks.
